# Analysis: "The Decade in Oncology" PDF - Tone, Language & Structure

## Executive Summary
The Liang Chang reference document is a **venture/investor-focused analysis masquerading as academic research**. It blends clinical precision with conversational narrative, using technical jargon strategically while remaining accessible to biotech decision-makers. This is **distinctly different from pure academic writing** and should inform your AACR 2026 report's positioning.

## 1. WRITING TONE AND VOICE

### Classification
**Primary tone: Conversational-analytical with venture-focused pragmatism**  
Secondary tones: Confident, contrarian, narrative-driven

### Sample Sentences That Define the Tone

1. **Personal, relatable opening:**
   > "Catching up with oncology is like trying to board a bullet train that left the station 15 years ago. Everything moves so fast."

2. **Colloquial with clinical precision:**
   > "For many of these patients, we are talking about functional cures."

3. **Opinionated analysis (investment thesis framing):**
   > "The divergence between heme and solid tumor outcomes is the defining structural feature of modern oncology, and in my opinion, it is driven by target biology that defines therapeutic index."

4. **Pragmatic, directional language:**
   > "The rule of thumb: if a drug's rung-1 data does not make oncologists say 'this obviously needs to be tested earlier,' it probably never will be."

5. **Candid about failures:**
   > "Almost all of them failed." (regarding checkpoint inhibitors)

### Key Characteristics
- **First-person perspective** ("I used 30 AI agents," "in my opinion")
- **Narrative framing** (describes events as a story with winners/losers)
- **Contrarian positioning** (explicitly argues against conventional wisdom)
- **Casual language embedded in technical content** ("bullet train," "graveyard," "tsunami")
- **Direct address to reader** (implies partnership in decision-making)

## 2. TECHNICAL LANGUAGE & JARGON

### Assessment
**Uses specialized biotech/VC jargon extensively BUT in service of accessibility**
- Assumes oncology baseline knowledge
- Does NOT explain basic terms (PFS, ORR, mOS assumed known)
- Introduces proprietary/strategic language frequently
- Strategic term usage that signals investment/business implications

### Key Terms & Examples (5-10 most telling)

| Term | Context | Implication |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **"The ladder"** | "Practice-changing drug...climbed a ladder from last-line...to first-line" | VC lens: market expansion = value creation |
| **"Rung-1 data"** | Analyzing early stage efficacy as predictor of commercial success | Strategic decision-making framework |
| **"Functional cures"** | Describing long-term PFS in blood cancers | De facto reframing of partial remission as success |
| **"Dispensable lineage"** | B cells can be ablated without killing patient | Technical + biological constraint → therapeutic opportunity |
| **"Bar movement"** | Improvements in OS/PFS metrics | Market-friendly language for clinical outcomes |
| **"The heme toolkit compounds"** | CAR-T + bispecifics + ADCs stacking | Portfolio/ecosystem thinking (VC language) |
| **"Dynasty 1, 2, 3"** | Phases of ADC development | Narrative structure that implies market evolution |
| **"Window"** | 2016-2020 IO revolution period | Temporal constraint framing (urgent opportunity window) |
| **"Phase 3 failure rate...near-universal"** | Checkpoint inhibitor outcomes | Quantified risk assessment |
| **"The graveyard"** | Failed post-PD-1 checkpoint programs | Emotionally-loaded term for losses |

### Speech Pattern
- **Heavy use of colon lists** ("Three structural advantages: dispensable lineage, clean surface antigen, modality advantage")
- **Statistical precision mixed with narrative language** ("64% single-year increase" + "The ADC Tsunami")
- **Drug names + trial names as shorthand** (assumes reader familiarity with KEYNOTE-024, FLAURA, etc.)
- **Acronyms without definition** (mOS, mPFS, ORR, TKI, ADC, TCE all used without glossary)

This document avoids all of it, positioning itself as **thoughtful analysis** rather than **research publication**.

## 4. RESULTS/FINDINGS PRESENTATION

### How Results Are Framed
**Clinical metrics (OS/PFS) paired with investment thesis language**

### Presentation Pattern 1: Clinical Outcomes with Strategic Implications
```
"CheckMate 214 9-year final analysis at ASCO 2025 confirmed durable benefit 
(ITT mOS 53 mo, intermediate/poor-risk subset 55.7 mo). And notably, the 
favorable-risk OS HR finally turned positive at 0.80"
```
- Provides exact survival data (clinical)
- Adds editorial commentary about timing ("finally turned positive")
- Interpretation frames this as a business validation, not just a clinical win

### Presentation Pattern 2: Comparative Framing (Winners vs. Losers)
```
"A treatment-naive CLL patient on zanubrutinib has roughly an 80% chance of being 
progression-free at 5 years...A metastatic pancreatic cancer patient receives 
FOLFIRINOX and lives 11.1 months"
```
- Juxtaposes success and failure
- Uses contrast to build argument
- Includes confidence language ("roughly") + precise numbers

### Presentation Pattern 3: Quantified Growth as Proxy for Importance
```
"ADC abstracts grew from ~50 in 2015 to ~290 in 2024, with ~330+ projected at 
ASCO 2025. ~6.6× growth"
```
- Abstract count = market momentum signal
- Growth rate more important than absolute efficacy
- Uses multipliers (6.6×) rather than percentages (more dramatic)

### Presentation Pattern 4: Failure Described with Color/Narrative
```
"And then there was the graveyard. Everyone assumed PD-1 and CTLA-4 would be the 
beginning, not the end. The industry launched enormous programs on IDO, TIGIT, 
LAG-3, TIM-3, VISTA, CD40L. Almost all of them failed."
```
- Emotional framing ("graveyard," "the comeback story that wasn't")
- Suggests **moral lesson** (hubris → failure)
- Not neutral scientific presentation

### Statistics vs. Investment Implications
- **Always provides exact numbers** (mOS 53 mo, HR 0.80, ORR 68%)
- **But frames these as market signals**, not just efficacy
- **Rarely uses confidence intervals or p-values** (when present: "p=0.0245")
- **No survival curves or detailed stat tables**
- **Emphasizes magnitude of effect** ("one of the largest bar movements in HER2+ breast cancer history")

### Section Structure Within Each Finding
1. **Headline (strategic framing)**
   - "Finding 1: There Are Two Oncologies, and the Gap Is Widening"
2. **Data presentation (specific trials/metrics)**
   - Lists trial names, survival data, response rates
3. **Narrative interpretation (why it matters)**
   - "Blood cancers have three structural advantages"
4. **Pattern extraction (rule derivation)**
   - "The rule of thumb: if a drug's rung-1 data does not make oncologists..."

### Headings & Visual Organization
- **Minimal heading hierarchy** (6 main findings, but no sub-sections)
- **Generous use of whitespace** (implies this is a long-form essay, not a technical document)
- **Bolded key concepts** ("The Winners," "The Stuck," "Dynasty 1, 2, 3")
- **No data tables** (all information embedded in narrative prose)
- **No figures or charts** (purely text-based)

### Flow Quality
- **Non-linear argument structure** (each finding is somewhat standalone)
- **Heavy forward-referencing** ("As I'm still distilling the long list of the remaining ones!")
- **Implicit narrative arc**: "Here's the past decade" → "Here's what we learned" → "Here's what's emerging"

## 6. TARGET AUDIENCE

### Primary Intended Reader
**Biotech/VC decision-makers with oncology knowledge** (not MDs or basic scientists)

### Evidence From Document
1. **Assumes oncology literacy**
   - No definitions of PFS, ORR, mOS, OS, HR
   - Trial names used as shorthand (CheckMate 214, KEYNOTE-024, FLAURA, EV-302)
   - Assumes familiarity with ASCO/ESMO meeting structure
   - Refers to "the decade" as a cohesive oncology narrative (requires insider perspective)

2. **Investment-decision framing**
   - "The speed of the ladder is the best predictive signal" → portfolio strategy implication
   - "Don't write a drug off entirely until the entire combinatorial space has been explored" → R&D thesis
   - "ADC era has arrived and is still accelerating. If I had to bet on the modality..." → directional thesis

3. **Risk-aware but opportunity-focused**
   - "The phase 3 failure rate for new checkpoint targets...near-universal"
   - But: "The lurbinectedin exception is humbling" (acknowledges that failure forecasting can fail)
   - Tone: pragmatic without cynicism

4. **Secondary audiences (implied but not addressed)**
   - Oncology researchers (will recognize the data but may find tone too casual)
   - Physicians (will understand clinical content but miss the strategic intent)
   - General biotech professionals (will be confused by lack of defined terms)

## 7. SYNTHESIS: ACADEMIC VS. JARGONNY

### This Document Is BOTH Academic AND Jargonny (Intentionally)

| Dimension | Academic | Jargonny | This Doc |
|-----------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Data foundation** | Peer-reviewed, validated | Proprietary/speculative | ✓ Academic (ASCO abstracts, public data) |
| **Citations** | Formal references | None | ✗ No formal citations, but trial names serve same function |
| **Methodology** | Detailed, reproducible | Opaque | ✗ Deliberately vague ("~30 AI agents") |
| **Language register** | Formal, impersonal | Casual, insider | ✓ Heavily colloquial throughout |
| **Argument structure** | Deductive/inductive rigor | Narrative persuasion | ✓ Narrative-driven with pattern extraction |
| **Error acknowledgment** | Limitations section | None | ✗ No limitations; few caveats |
| **Confidence hedging** | Extensive ("may," "suggest," "indicates") | Minimal | ✗ Very confident in assertions |
| **Strategic framing** | Absent | Central | ✓ Every finding has investment implication |

### The Verdict for Your AACR Report
**This document is "less academic" but not "less rigorous"**

It achieves credibility through:
1. **Precise data citations** (specific trials, exact survival numbers)
2. **Pattern recognition across large dataset** (57,603 abstracts)
3. **Logical argument structure** (findings 1-6 build coherently)
4. **Intellectual honesty about failures** ("The graveyard," "near-universal failure rate")

But it sacrifices academic conventions:
1. **No methodology section** → audiences must trust the author
2. **No formal statistical testing** → depends on effect sizes being visually obvious
3. **Editorial interpretation baked in** → no neutral presentation of facts
4. **Narrative over exhaustive documentation** → faster to read but harder to replicate

## 8. KEY STYLISTIC DIFFERENCES TO GUIDE YOUR AACR REPORT

### If Your Goal Is "Less Jargonny, More Academic"
**This reference is the wrong direction.** It's MORE jargonny (intentionally), not less.

### What This Doc Does That Makes It Work:
✅ Uses jargon **purposefully** (every acronym serves the argument)  
✅ Pairs jargon with **narrative context** ("The Ladder Is the Best Predictive Sign" explains WHY drug climbing matters before using the term)  
✅ **Defines jargon through usage**, not explicitly (reader learns "rung" means therapeutic line through examples)  
✅ Maintains **conversational voice** throughout (never sounds like a dry technical manual)  
✅ Balances **precision** (exact numbers) with **accessibility** (story-like structure)  

### How to Use This as a Reference
**If you want your AACR report to be "less jargonny":**
- Reduce unexplained acronyms (add glossary for first mention)
- Include more basic-science context for clinical findings
- Use more transition sentences between concepts
- Define "why this matters" for each finding

**If you want it to remain jargonny but more "academic":**
- Add a formal Methods section (even briefly)
- Include confidence intervals or statistical tests where possible
- Add a Limitations section
- Use third-person voice instead of first-person
- Add formal References section
- Remove emotional/narrative framing ("graveyard," "tsunami," "catching up like a bullet train")
